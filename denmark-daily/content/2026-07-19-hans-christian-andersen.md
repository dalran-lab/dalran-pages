He signed himself **H.C. Andersen**, and outside Denmark he is often mistaken for a folklorist who merely collected old tales. In fact **Hans Christian Andersen** (1805–1875) invented his stories — and in doing so became the most translated Danish author in history, read today in more than 150 languages.

He was born in **Odense**, on the island of **Funen (Fyn)**, into deep poverty: his father was a shoemaker, his mother a washerwoman who later died an alcoholic. At **14** he left for **Copenhagen** alone, chasing a childish dream of the theatre. He failed as a singer and an actor, but his obvious talent won him a patron — the influential official **Jonas Collin**, who secured him a royal grant and a belated grammar-school education.

What Andersen produced was a new kind of literary fairy tale (*eventyr*): colloquial, ironic and emotionally raw. **The Little Mermaid** (1837), **The Ugly Duckling**, **The Emperor's New Clothes**, **The Snow Queen**, **The Little Match Girl**, **Thumbelina** and **The Princess and the Pea** all carry a double voice — enchantment for children, sly social commentary for adults. The Ugly Duckling, the misfit who turns out to be a swan, is nearly autobiographical.

His most visible legacy sits on a rock at **Langelinie** in Copenhagen: the bronze **Little Mermaid** (*Den lille Havfrue*), sculpted by **Edvard Eriksen**, unveiled in **1913** and commissioned by brewer **Carl Jacobsen** of Carlsberg. Barely 1.25 metres tall and often underwhelming to first-time visitors, she is nonetheless the most photographed object in Denmark.

Andersen's climb from a washerwoman's son to a friend of kings, fêted across Europe, made him a living emblem of social mobility — the idea, central to Denmark's self-image, that talent should be free to rise. He died in 1875 and is buried at **Assistens Cemetery** in the Nørrebro district of Copenhagen. In **2021** his home city opened a striking new **H.C. Andersen Museum**, designed by the Japanese architect **Kengo Kuma**, a garden-like labyrinth built to feel like stepping into one of his tales.
