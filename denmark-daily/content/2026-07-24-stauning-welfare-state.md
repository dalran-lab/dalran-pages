Every welfare state has its founding fathers. Denmark's wears a vast beard and a permanent cigar: **Thorvald Stauning** (1873–1942), the cigar-sorter's son who became the country's first Social Democratic prime minister and, more than anyone, built the political machine that became the modern Danish state.

Stauning rose through the trade-union movement and led the **Social Democrats** (*Socialdemokratiet*) into power, serving as prime minister in 1924–26 and again from 1929 until his death in 1942. His defining moment came in the depths of the Great Depression. On a January night in **1933**, with unemployment soaring and farmers in crisis, he gathered rival party leaders in his own Copenhagen apartment and hammered out the **Kanslergade Agreement** (*Kanslergadeforliget*), named after the street where he lived.

The deal was a masterpiece of Danish consensus politics. It devalued the krone to help farmers and exporters, banned strikes and lockouts to keep social peace, funded public works — and cleared the way for the sweeping **Social Reform of 1933**, drafted by minister **K.K. Steincke**, which fused a tangle of poor-relief laws into a coherent system of social *rights*. It is often seen as the seed of the universal welfare state.

Stauning also gave Danish politics one of its most famous slogans. As Nazism rose abroad, the Social Democrats fought the **1935 election** under the banner *"Stauning eller Kaos"* — "Stauning or Chaos" — and won a landslide, a rare burst of personality politics in a consensus culture.

He died in office in 1942, during the German occupation, mourned as a national father figure. His legacy is everywhere in today's Denmark: the Social Democrats remain one of the two dominant parties and produced the current prime minister, **Mette Frederiksen**, in office since 2019. The instinct Stauning embodied — that bitter rivals should sit down and compromise for the common good — is still, arguably, the deepest reflex in Danish political life.
