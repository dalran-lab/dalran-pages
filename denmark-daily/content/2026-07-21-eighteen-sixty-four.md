Ask a Dane which year turned their country into the small, cohesive nation it is today, and many will answer **1864**.

At the start of the 19th century Denmark was still a middling European power, ruling the duchies of **Schleswig, Holstein and Lauenburg** on its southern border. But these lands mixed Danish- and German-speaking populations, and as nationalism swept Europe the question of who owned them became explosive. When Copenhagen tried to bind Schleswig more tightly to the kingdom, the Prussian minister-president **Otto von Bismarck** seized the pretext.

In 1864 the armies of **Prussia and Austria** invaded. The decisive blow fell at the **Battle of Dybbøl on 18 April 1864**, where entrenched Danish troops were pulverised by superior Prussian artillery. The defeat was total. In the peace that followed, Denmark lost all three duchies — roughly **40% of its territory and population** at a single stroke.

The trauma reshaped the national psyche. A country that had imagined itself a small great power now understood itself as simply small. Out of that humiliation came a famous, forward-looking slogan associated with the heath-reclaiming engineer **Enrico Mylius Dalgas**: *"Hvad udad tabes, skal indad vindes"* — "What is lost without shall be won within." Denmark turned inward and downward, cultivating the barren heaths of Jutland, building cooperatives and folk high schools, and investing in its own people rather than in territory.

The story has a partial epilogue. After Germany's defeat in the First World War, a **1920 plebiscite** let **Northern Schleswig (Sønderjylland)** vote itself back into Denmark — the **Genforening**, or Reunification, still commemorated today. The border drawn in 1920 left a Danish minority in Germany and a German minority in Denmark who now coexist as a model of peaceful minority relations.

Denmark's reckoning with 1864 continues in culture, most prominently in **Ole Bornedal's 2014 television epic *1864***, one of the most expensive Danish productions ever made. The war remains the hinge on which modern Danish identity turns.
