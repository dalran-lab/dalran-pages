Denmark's Birth Certificate — The Jelling Stones

In a churchyard in the small Jutland town of Jelling stand two carved granite stones that Danes call the nation's dåbsattest — its baptismal certificate. They mark the moment, more than a thousand years ago, when a scattered people first appears in the record as "Denmark."

The smaller stone was raised by King Gorm the Old (Gorm den Gamle) around 950 in memory of his wife, Queen Thyra. The larger, more famous stone was commissioned about 965 by their son, Harald Bluetooth (Harald Blåtand), in memory of both parents. Its runic inscription is boastful and precise: Harald, it says, "won for himself all Denmark and Norway and made the Danes Christian." Carved on its side is one of the earliest Nordic images of Christ — tangled not on a cross but in the interlacing ribbons of Viking art, a pagan world visibly turning Christian.

Three things make the stones extraordinary. They contain the first use of the name Danmark on Danish soil; they record the unification of the kingdom under a single ruler; and they announce a top-down religious conversion that pulled Denmark into Christian Europe. The whole site — two great burial mounds, the runestones, and a later stone church — is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Harald's reach extends, improbably, into your pocket. When engineers in the 1990s needed a name for a wireless standard meant to unite competing devices, they borrowed the king who had united Denmark: Bluetooth. Its logo is a bind-rune fusing Harald's initials, ᚼ (H) and ᛒ (B).

You can still see the original larger stone in Jelling, now sealed inside a climate-controlled glass case installed in 2011 to slow a thousand years of weathering. A vivid reconstruction shows how it once blazed with red, black and gold paint — a reminder that the austere grey monuments of the Viking Age were, in their day, loud with colour and ambition.
