The Chair as National Art — Danish Modern

No country has made more of a simple chair than Denmark. In the mid-20th century a small circle of designers in Copenhagen turned furniture into one of the nation's great cultural exports — and into a way of life now bound up with the word hygge.

The movement's intellectual father was Kaare Klint, who founded the furniture department at the Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts in 1924 and taught designers to study the human body, proportion and historical forms before touching a saw. His students and successors made the results famous.

Hans J. Wegner (1914–2007), a trained cabinetmaker, designed close to 500 chairs, among them the Wishbone Chair (1949) and the Round Chair — so admired it is simply called "The Chair," and famously used by John F. Kennedy and Richard Nixon in their 1960 televised debate. Arne Jacobsen (1902–1971), an architect as much as a designer, created the sculptural Egg and Swan chairs in 1958 for his total design of the SAS Royal Hotel in Copenhagen — the world's first "designer hotel," down to the cutlery. Others completed the constellation: Finn Juhl, Børge Mogensen, and lighting designer Poul Henningsen, whose glare-free PH lamps still glow in Danish homes.

What united them was a philosophy: honest materials (teak, oak, leather), craftsmanship inherited from the Copenhagen Cabinetmakers' Guild, organic forms shaped to the body, and the democratic conviction that good design should be for everyone, not just the rich. Function and beauty were meant to be one and the same.

The style conquered the world — especially America, where "Danish Modern" became shorthand for tasteful mid-century living. And it never went away. Firms such as Fritz Hansen and Carl Hansen & Søn still produce the original designs; a Wegner or Jacobsen chair remains an aspirational object worldwide.

To see the whole story, visit Designmuseum Danmark in Copenhagen, whose corridor of 20th-century chairs is a pilgrimage site for designers. It is also the clearest window into the Danish belief that the objects of everyday life deserve to be beautiful.
